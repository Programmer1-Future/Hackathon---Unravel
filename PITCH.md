## 2. The Main Pitch (2–3 minutes)

**The problem is real, and it's massive.**

One in five students is neurodivergent — ADHD, dyslexia, executive dysfunction. For them, studying isn't held back by *intelligence*. It's held back by **friction and overwhelm.** A dense page isn't information — it's a threat. The hardest part of studying isn't understanding the material. It's *starting.*

**Why don't existing tools fix this?**

NotebookLM, ChatGPT — genuinely powerful. But watch what they actually demand. Leave your page. Open a new tab. Copy the text — oh wait, it's a PDF, so *screenshot* it. Upload it. Type your question. Read back a wall of text in a *different* wall of text.

That's not one problem. That's six moments of friction — and a distractible brain only has to slip on one of them to give up entirely. These tools were built for focused adults. They were **never built for the brain that needs them most.**

**Unravel is built the other way around.**

It lives *on the page.* Highlight anything — a paragraph, a definition, a proof — and a floating **Unravel** button appears. Tap it, and a side panel opens with a **concept map that reveals itself one layer at a time.** You don't get the whole tree. You get the *one core idea* first. Tap it — its children unfold. Tap again — one more layer. Colour-coded: concept, definition, example, formula. Plain language, always.

This is the heart of it. **You are never shown a wall of text again.** Progressive disclosure isn't a UI trick here — it's the entire anti-overwhelm mechanic, built around how an ADHD brain actually takes information in. Small. Calm. Conquerable.

**Then there's the part nobody else can do.**

Students don't study from clean web pages. They study from **PDFs** — Physics & Maths Tutor, past papers, textbook scans. And here's the wall inside the wall: **Chrome physically blocks extensions from reading its own PDF viewer.** Most study tools just... can't touch the exact files students use every single day.

So Unravel doesn't read the text. **It *sees* the page.** We screenshot the tab and send the pixels straight to Gemini's multimodal vision — and get back the same clean concept map. PDFs. Diagrams. A photo of your own handwritten notes. If you can see it, Unravel can unravel it. **This only exists because Gemini can see.**

**And once you understand something, Unravel makes sure it sticks — and feels good.**

Finish exploring a section and a 30-second **micro-quest** pops up. Answer right? **Confetti. XP.** Passive reading becomes an active loop — the exact dopamine reward an executive-dysfunction brain is starving for. *Beat the section to move on.* Hit a formula like projectile motion, and Unravel builds a live **sandbox** — Gemini pulls out the variables, and you get **sliders driving a graph that morphs in real time.** You don't read the physics. You *play* it. And a **Coach chat** sits there the whole time, already holding the entire page as context — no pasting, no uploading, just ask, and get a short, plain answer back.

All of it wrapped in XP, streaks, and a dashboard that turns revision into a Duolingo-style habit. Built with OpenDyslexic, reduced-motion, light and dark, and a warm tactile design that feels like a **friendly toy, not a clinical tool.** Accessibility isn't a checkbox here. It's the whole point.

**Why Unravel wins.**

Other teams built clever tech. We built the thing a real, overwhelmed student can open *tonight* — on the actual PDF in front of them — and feel the wall come down. **Impressive. Useful. Shippable. Today.**

## 5. If Judges Ask (Q&A prep)

**"How is this different from NotebookLM or ChatGPT?"**
> Two things. **Where it lives, and who it's for.** They make you leave your material — copy, paste, screenshot, switch apps. Unravel lives *on* the page you're already reading; zero context-switching. And every design choice — progressive disclosure, plain-language, OpenDyslexic, reduced-motion, the reward loop — is built specifically for neurodivergent brains. They give you answers. **We remove the friction and the overwhelm that stop those brains from starting.**

**"Does it *really* use Gemini, or is that a label?"**
> Deeply. **Five distinct Gemini 2.5 Flash call types**, each using Gemini-specific features: text → structured concept tree via `responseSchema` JSON; multimodal vision → concept tree from a screenshot; the Coach → long-context whole-page plus a multimodal image with system instructions; quiz generation → structured JSON; and the sandbox → a parameterised math model pulled from prose. The **live indicator pulses on every real call** — you watched it fire throughout the demo. Not a wrapper. The engine.

**"Does it actually work on PDFs? Chrome blocks that."**
> Correct — Chrome blocks extensions from its PDF viewer, and that's exactly the wall we beat. We don't fight for the text. **We screenshot the tab and send the pixels to Gemini's vision.** You saw it run on a live PMT PDF. It works the same on diagrams and even a photo of handwritten notes. **This is only possible because Gemini can see** — it's our single biggest edge.

**"Is it safe? Sandbox running formulas sounds risky."**
> Every formula is evaluated with **mathjs — never `eval`.** Parsed, sandboxed, safe. No arbitrary code execution.

**"What's next?"**
> Spaced-repetition that resurfaces quests you struggled with. Shareable concept maps for study groups. Teacher and SEN-coordinator dashboards. And a mobile capture flow so a student can photograph a worksheet and unravel it anywhere. But the core is **built, working, and shippable today** — that's the point.
